## 1. The Core Principle

A project description is not a report. It is a story about change.

> **Before state → Tension → Insight → Solution → After state**

The reader should finish understanding:
1. Why this existed
2. What made it hard
3. What you figured out
4. What the outcome was

The copy does NOT need to be flashy. It needs to be clear, specific, and true.

---

## 2. The Project Description Structure

Use this structure for any project, regardless of complexity. Apply it loosely — not every section appears in every piece.

```
[HOOK LINE]
One sentence that captures the essence of the project. Should work as a headline.

[CONTEXT / PROBLEM]
What was the world like before this? What was broken, missing, or slow?

[THE INSIGHT]
What did you see that others didn't? What was the bet you made?

[THE SOLUTION]
What did you build? How does it work? (One level of depth — not a spec sheet.)

[THE RESULT]
Numbers if you have them. Changed behavior if you don't. Quote if possible.

[WHAT MADE IT HARD]
The honest constraint or creative challenge. This is what proves expertise.

[WHAT'S NEXT]
Optional. Only include if there's genuine forward momentum.
```

## 3. Section-by-Section Guidance

### Hook Line

This is the headline. It should work as a standalone statement.

**Rules**:
- Under 10 words
- Describe the change, not the tool
- Don't use the word "platform," "solution," or "system" unless unavoidable

**Weak**: "A platform for managing real estate listings"
**Strong**: "The fastest way for agents to list a property — by voice."

**Weak**: "We built a machine learning model."
**Strong**: "Our model predicts churn 60 days before it happens."

---

### Context / Problem

Set the scene. What was painful, inefficient, or broken?

**Rules**:
- Write in present tense ("The team spends..." not "The team spent...")
- Make the pain specific and real — don't abstract it
- One paragraph, 2–4 sentences

**Pattern**: `[Who] [struggles with / spends hours on] [specific task]. [The consequence is X]. [The existing options] fail because [specific reason].`

---

### The Insight

The "aha" moment. What did you understand that was not obvious?

**Rules**:
- This is NOT the solution. It's the lens through which you saw the problem differently.
- Keep it to 1–2 sentences.

**Excellent examples (real-world)**:
- "We realized the problem wasn't the lack of data — it was the 11-second load time that made agents stop checking."
- "The highest-intent users were abandoning at the form. Not because the form was hard. Because they didn't trust us yet."

---

### The Solution

Describe what was built. One level of depth — not a spec sheet.

**Rules**:
- Focus on what the user experiences, not what the system does
- Lead with the outcome, then the mechanism
- Avoid jargon unless your reader is technical

**Pattern**: `[User] can now [do X] by [simple description of how]. The system [key technical mechanism] behind the scenes.`

---

### The Result

This is the most important section. Be specific.

**Hierarchy** (use the highest level available):
1. Revenue / cost impact
2. Behavior change metrics (retention, activation, NPS)
3. Operational efficiency (time saved, errors reduced)
4. Usage data (DAU, completion rates)
5. Qualitative shift (team described it as...)

**Rules**:
- Real numbers > percentages > adjectives
- If you can't share numbers, describe the *type* of change
- Don't say "significantly" — say how much

---

### What Made It Hard

The honest acknowledgment of constraint. This is what separates a project description from a press release.

**Examples of things that made it hard**:
- Technical: "We had to work with a legacy database without read-write separation"
- Design: "Every user had a different mental model of what 'ready' meant"
- Organizational: "Three teams had to align on a single data definition"
- Time: "We shipped a working prototype in 4 days to meet the launch window"

## 4. Tone Calibration by Audience

| Audience | Tone | Lead With |
|---|---|---|
| **Investors** | Confident, precise | Market size, traction, moat |
| **Hiring managers** | Credible, specific | Your role, your decision, your impact |
| **Clients / customers** | Clear, warm | The problem you solved *for them* |
| **Engineers** | Direct, technical | Architecture decisions, tradeoffs made |
| **Designers** | Visual, process-oriented | The problem, the iteration, the reasoning |
| **Press / media** | Narrative, accessible | The human story, the unexpected angle |
| **General portfolio** | Balanced | Hook line + result + one surprising detail |

## 5. The Project Logline

For every project, write a **logline** — a single sentence that would make someone ask "tell me more."

**Structure**: `[Role or product] that [unique mechanism] so [audience] can [outcome].`

**Examples**:
- "An AI that reads doctor notes aloud so patients understand their diagnosis before they leave the room."
- "A Figma plugin that generates accessible contrast-passing color palettes in one click."
- "A Shopify app that turns Instagram comments into completed orders."

**Apple-translated version** (for premium contexts):
`[Short punchy claim]. [One sentence expansion.] [Optional: what made it possible.]`

- "Medicine, translated." / "Patients hear their diagnosis in plain language — directly from the doctor's notes."
- "Listings, at the speed of voice." / "Agents describe a property once. The listing is ready in seconds."

## 6. The Technical Project Makeover

When the user has a technical project, apply this transformation to make it human:

| Raw technical description | Copywritten version |
|---|---|
| "Built a NLP pipeline with BERT fine-tuning" | "Trained our own model to understand industry-specific language — so search actually works." |
| "Implemented Redis caching layer" | "Reduced page load from 4 seconds to 400 milliseconds." |
| "Designed a microservices architecture" | "Each part of the system can be updated without touching the rest." |
| "Wrote unit tests with 95% coverage" | "The system has caught 47 bugs before users ever saw them." |
| "Integrated with 12 third-party APIs" | "Works with the tools your team already uses — no migration required." |

**Rule**: Always answer "so what?" after every technical claim. The technical detail is the proof. The human outcome is the point.

## 7. Anti-Patterns to Avoid

These make project descriptions sound generic or corporate. Never use:

| Phrase | Replace with |
|---|---|
| "We leveraged..." | "We used..." |
| "Seamless experience" | Describe what feels seamless specifically |
| "Innovative solution" | Say what it does. Innovation is a judgment — let the reader decide. |
| "Robust platform" | Name the scale, speed, or reliability metric |
| "User-centric design" | "We tested with 12 users before shipping any feature" |
| "Cutting-edge" | Name the specific technology or approach |
| "Scalable architecture" | "Handles 10,000 concurrent users on a $50/month instance" |
| "End-to-end" | Say what the ends are specifically |
| "Best-in-class" | Compared to what? Give the comparison. |
| "Passionate team" | Show the passion through a specific decision or sacrifice |

## 8. Project Description Examples

### Example A: Developer Tool (Short Form)

**Hook**: Dependency hell, solved.

**Problem**: Every time a dependency breaks, developers spend hours figuring out why. The error messages aren't helpful. The docs are outdated. The fix is usually one line — finding it takes an afternoon.

**Insight**: The answer already exists in someone's commit history. We just needed to surface it.

**Solution**: A CLI tool that scans your error context, finds the exact commit where a similar fix was made across public repos, and shows you the diff — in your terminal, in seconds.

**Result**: Beta users reported saving an average of 2.3 hours per debugging session. One team cut their dependency-related sprint overruns by 60%.

---

### Example B: SaaS Product (Apple-Style)

**Hook line**: The deal room. Reinvented.

**Body**:
Real estate transactions involve dozens of people, hundreds of documents, and a timeline nobody can see. Deals collapse because of miscommunication — not because the deal is bad.

We built a single environment where every stakeholder — agent, buyer, lender, attorney — works from the same version of truth. Documents trigger the right next step automatically. Delays surface before they become crises.

The result: transactions that once took 47 days now close in 31. With fewer calls. And far fewer surprises.

---

### Example C: Internal Tool (Brief, hiring context)

**Hook**: Onboarding that used to take 3 weeks. Now takes 3 days.

Built an internal knowledge base with semantic search across 4,000 support tickets, internal docs, and Slack threads. New hires find answers to novel questions without waiting for a Slack response.

My role: led the data pipeline design and wrote the search ranking layer from scratch. The hardest part: getting alignment across 6 teams on what "authoritative" meant before we could build anything.

Outcome: median time-to-first-resolution for new support reps dropped from 18 minutes to 4 minutes. Still in use 14 months after launch.
