## 1. The Compound Boast

**Pattern**: `[Adjective]. [Adjective]. [Noun phrase].`

Stacks two short quality claims before naming the payoff.

**Apple examples**:
- "Thin. Fast. Powerful and portable." (MacBook Air)
- "Feature stacked. Value packed." (iPhone 17e)
- "Smart. Secure. On device." (Apple Intelligence)

**When to use**: Product hero copy where you need to communicate multiple qualities without over-writing. Great for taglines under product names.

**Template**: `[Quality A]. [Quality B]. [What it does / what it is].`

---

## 2. The Adjective Mashup (Portmanteau Play)

**Pattern**: Invent a word by fusing two concepts that describe the product's dual nature.

**Apple examples**:
- "Magichromatic." (Apple Watch Ultra 2 — magic + chromatic)
- "Now supercharged by M5." (hint at a transformation, not just spec)

**When to use**: For hero headlines on premium or novel products where you want to create a distinctive brand moment. High-risk, high-reward — only use when the fusion is obvious and delightful.

**Template**: `[Concept A][Concept B]-matic / -ify / -chrome / -sonic.`

## 3. The Superlative Claim

**Pattern**: "The [superlative] [noun] [qualifier]."

Makes a bold, singular claim. Works best when backed by a real proof point in the subhead.

**Apple examples**:
- "The thinnest iPhone ever."
- "The world's best in-ear Active Noise Cancellation."
- "The ultimate theater. Wherever you are."
- "The most advanced Mac laptops for demanding tasks."
- "Our most advanced Spatial Audio system ever."

**When to use**: For category-defining products or breakthrough features. Always follow with a specific proof point or it sounds hollow.

**Template**: `The [most/best/first/only] [noun] [context].`

---

## 4. The Contrast Move

**Pattern**: Acknowledge the old, name the new. Create a "before/after" in one or two lines.

**Apple examples**:
- "Give us the old. Save on the new." (Apple Trade In)
- "New look. Even more magic." (iOS and Apple Intelligence)
- "Switching from Android to iPhone is simple."
- "Switch to iPhone."

**When to use**: When targeting switchers or upgrade-eligible users. Excellent for Trade In, upgrade, or migration campaigns.

**Template**: `[What they have]. [What they get].` or `[Old state] is over. [New state] is here.`

---

## 5. The Declaration

**Pattern**: A complete, bold sentence that asserts a world-changing truth. Short. Definitive. No qualifiers.

**Apple examples**:
- "The era of spatial computing is here."
- "The start of a new era for Siri."
- "iPhone for all."
- "Innovation you can see, hear, and feel."

**When to use**: For launch moments, category announcements, or to open a new section with gravitas.

**Template**: `[Era/world/product] [verb phrase].` — Keep under 8 words.

---

## 6. The Rhythm Triple

**Pattern**: Three parallel verb phrases or short sentences in rapid succession. Read aloud — it should feel like a groove.

**Apple examples**:
- "Walk it. Talk it. Track it. Love it." (Apple Watch)
- "Hey Siri, set my bedtime scene / make it warmer / turn off the lights downstairs"
- "Stream. Play. Create." (implied in services copy)
- "Create, communicate, and get things done effortlessly."

**When to use**: For lifestyle or feature-listing sections. The rhythm communicates momentum and comprehensiveness without feeling like a spec list.

**Template**: `[Verb] [object]. [Verb] [object]. [Verb] [object].` (3–4 beats)

---

## 7. The Command

**Pattern**: A short imperative verb phrase that sounds confident, not pushy.

**Apple examples**:
- "Go fast. Go far." (MacBook performance)
- "Dream it up. Jot it down." (iPad + Apple Pencil)
- "Power. Fully. Fast." (iPhone charging)
- "Kick back. Tune in. Game on." (iPad entertainment)

**When to use**: For feature callouts in the body of a page, or as a lead into a section. Works especially well for lifestyle moments.

**Template**: `[Verb] [adverb/direction]. [Verb] [adverb/direction].`

---

## 8. The Specificity Drop

**Pattern**: A precise number or technical detail that builds instant credibility.

**Apple examples**:
- "More pixels than a 4K TV. For each eye." (Apple Vision Pro)
- "23 million pixels" (Vision Pro display)
- "Up to 33 hours of video playback on iPhone 17 Pro"
- "95 percent recycled lithium in the battery"
- "One billion pixels per second"
- "Up to 4x more Active Noise Cancellation"

**When to use**: In proof sections, feature callouts, or whenever you make a superlative claim that needs grounding. The number does the persuasion — let it stand alone.

**Template**: Stat → plain-language impact. E.g., "X metric. What it means for you."

---

## 9. The Identity Line

**Pattern**: Copy that defines the reader's self-image. It says "this product is for someone like you."

**Apple examples**:
- "Your business is nobody else's." (Mac Privacy)
- "Your workplace can be any place." (iPad)
- "Your classroom can be anywhere." (iPad education)
- "iPhone for all."
- "Take your inner artist out and about."

**When to use**: When speaking to a specific lifestyle, profession, or value set. Extremely effective for iPad (creator, student, professional) and iPhone (photographer, parent, adventurer).

**Template**: `Your [noun] can be [redefined context].` or `[Possessive pronoun] + [aspiration].`

---

## 10. The Understatement

**Pattern**: Describe something extraordinary in subtly restrained language. The gap between the claim and the tone is the hook.

**Apple examples**:
- "The magic of Mac at a surprising price."
- "Profound sound." (HomePod)
- "Surprising sound for its size." (HomePod mini)
- "A sight to be bold." (Apple Watch Ultra)

**When to use**: For premium products where over-selling would feel cheap. Works when the product's reputation does the heavy lifting.

**Template**: `[Modest adjective] [noun].` Let the noun carry the weight.

---

## 11. The Rhyme / Sound Pattern

**Pattern**: Use near-rhyme, alliteration, or sonic mirroring to make copy stick.

**Apple examples**:
- "Loud and clear." (Audio)
- "Fast that lasts." (iPhone chip + battery)
- "Easy to use. Easy to love." (Mac)
- "Pixel sharpener." (display alliteration)
- "Significant others." (accessories section)

**When to use**: For headlines and section separators where memorability is the goal. These are the lines users repeat to each other.

**Template**: `[A word]. [A rhyme/echo].` — Simple, tight, memorable.

---

## 12. The Era Announcement

**Pattern**: Position the product as a historical turning point. Future-facing, cultural, definitive.

**Apple examples**:
- "The era of spatial computing is here."
- "Revolutionary. Now powered by M5."
- "A whole new element of delight."
- "An exciting new platform. A world of new opportunities."

**When to use**: For category-first products, major chip generations, or platform announcements. Reserve this — overuse dilutes it.

**Template**: `[Adjective]. [Category noun].` or `The era of [category] is [here/now/beginning].`

---

## 13. The Feature-Benefit Pair

**Pattern**: Name the feature → Immediately explain the human outcome. Two sentences, maximum.

**Apple examples**:
- "Active Noise Cancellation reduces unwanted sounds so you can immerse yourself in what you're listening to or simply stay focused."
- "Personalized Spatial Audio brings you 3D sound suited to your specific ear shape for a theater-like experience."
- "With Find My, you can securely share your location with friends and family."
- "Private Browsing in Safari locks browsing windows when they're not being used, blocks known trackers..."

**When to use**: In the body of any product page, especially in feature detail sections. Never list features without a benefit — always follow the pair.

**Template**: `[Feature name] [verb] [what it does] so you can [human outcome].`

---

## 14. The Concise Promise

**Pattern**: A single short sentence that is the entire value proposition. No qualifiers. No exclamation marks.

**Apple examples**:
- "Help is here."
- "Here and wow."
- "All out Pro."
- "Powerful alone. Superpowered together."
- "Handled with AppleCare."

**When to use**: As section openers, card headlines, or when a product's value is self-evident and the copy should get out of the way.

**Template**: `[Value state].` — Under 5 words. Trust the product.
