## 1. Quick-Select by Copy Goal

Use this table to quickly identify which psychological model to activate, based on what you want the reader to *do* or *feel*.

| Copy Goal | Primary Model | Secondary Model |
|---|---|---|
| Make the product feel premium | Authority Bias + Specificity | Anchoring |
| Overcome price hesitation | Loss Aversion | Anchoring (compare to worse option) |
| Drive sign-up / first use | Default Effect | Mere Exposure |
| Reduce switching friction | Status-Quo Bias (flip it) | IKEA Effect |
| Create urgency | Loss Aversion | Availability Heuristic |
| Build trust | Social Proof | Authority Bias |
| Simplify a complex choice | Paradox of Choice | Default Effect |
| Speak to aspiration | Identity (Jobs-to-Be-Done) | Endowment Effect |
| Write sustainability copy | Loss Framing | Social Proof (collective identity) |
| Write switching / migration copy | Loss Aversion (what they lose by not switching) | Framing Effect |
| Launch a new category | Availability Heuristic | Authority Bias |
| Write a hero headline | Framing Effect | Loss Aversion OR Identity |

## 2. Psychology → Copy Translation

How each model translates into specific word-level decisions in copy.

### Loss Aversion (PLFS baseline: 13–15 for most copy)
**The model**: People fear loss ~2x more than they value equivalent gain.

**In copy**:
- Frame the absence of the product as the loss: "Without it, you're still doing this the slow way."
- Use "miss out" framing instead of "gain" framing: "Don't miss the trade-in window."
- Contrast copy: "iPhone holds its value longer than other smartphones." (losing value = loss)

**Real Apple examples**:
- "New now. And later." (don't lose the future value you'll get from keeping iOS updated)
- "If your device isn't eligible for credit, we'll recycle it for free." (removes the fear of wasting the old device)

---

### Authority Bias (PLFS baseline: 10–13)
**The model**: People defer to perceived experts and systems of credibility.

**In copy**:
- Use specific numbers — they signal measurement, therefore expertise
- Name the technology, not just the outcome
- Use third-party proof (awards, press mentions, certifications)
- Apple's chip naming (M5, A18 Bionic) functions as authority

**Real Apple examples**:
- "23 million pixels" — the specificity signals expertise
- "Our disassembly robots — Daisy, Dave, and Taz —" — naming the robots makes them real
- "95 percent recycled lithium in the battery" — the precision earns trust

---

### Social Proof (PLFS baseline: 9–12)
**The model**: People look to others to validate their decisions.

**In copy**:
- Implied social proof: "The world's best in-ear Active Noise Cancellation" (implies global judgment)
- Named communities: "For doctors, agents, creators, students..."
- Volume signals: "Millions of shows..."
- Brand endorsement: Apple's use of F1, NBA, and award-winning shows

**Real Apple examples**:
- "The world's best in-ear Active Noise Cancellation." (superlative = consensus)
- "Stream award-winning Apple Originals on every screen."
- Partnership names in services (NYT, Joe Rogan, etc.) all function as social proof

---

### Framing Effect (PLFS baseline: 11–14)
**The model**: The same information presented differently produces different decisions.

**In copy**:
- Frame features as constraints removed, not capabilities added: "Your workplace can be any place." vs. "iPad works in the office."
- Frame price as investment, not cost: "Pay over time, interest-free."
- Frame trade-in as a gain, not giving up: "Get credit toward a new one."

**Real Apple examples**:
- "Give us the old. Save on the new." (reframe trade-in as saving, not selling)
- "Made for 5G." (frames 5G as native, not add-on)
- "Pay in full or pay over time." (choice framing removes sticker shock)

---

### Anchoring (PLFS baseline: 10–13)
**The model**: The first number or reference point becomes the standard against which everything else is judged.

**In copy**:
- Lead with the top-tier product or price, then show the accessible option
- Use the "compare to" technique: "More pixels than a 4K TV. For each eye."
- Dollar-value framing: "Get up to $1100 in credit..."

**Real Apple examples**:
- "More pixels than a 4K TV. For each eye." (4K TV is the anchor — Vision Pro exceeds it)
- "Up to $830–$1100 in credit on a new iPhone." (large number anchors perceived value of trade-in)

---

### Identity / Jobs-to-Be-Done (PLFS baseline: 12–14 for lifestyle copy)
**The model**: People buy products to make progress in their lives — to become the person they want to be.

**In copy**:
- Define the user's identity in the copy, not the product's identity
- "For people who..." lines create instant resonance
- Aspiration is more powerful than capability

**Real Apple examples**:
- "Take your inner artist out and about." (you are an artist)
- "Your workplace can be any place." (you are untethered)
- "Your classroom can be anywhere." (you are a learner, everywhere)
- "Last phone standing." (you make smart, lasting choices)

---

### Default Effect (PLFS baseline: 10–13)
**The model**: People tend to accept defaults — make the desired action the path of least resistance.

**In copy**:
- Make the recommended option linguistically obvious: "Recommended"
- Pre-select the right tier/option in pricing tables (applies to adjacent copy)
- Use language that assumes the action: "Your new iPhone." (not "if you buy an iPhone")

**Real Apple examples**:
- "Meet your new iPhone with Personal Setup." (assumes purchase has occurred)
- "Meet your new Mac with Personal Setup." (same — purchase is the default assumed state)

---

### Paradox of Choice (PLFS baseline: 11–14)
**The model**: More options reduce decision quality and confidence.

**In copy**:
- Offer a decision aid: "Help me choose." = brilliant copy that solves paradox of choice
- Use curated language: "The right Mac for you" vs. "All Macs"
- One clear CTA per section — never two competing actions

**Real Apple examples**:
- "Help me choose. Answer a few questions to find the best Mac for you." (converts choice paralysis into a guided experience)
- "Which AirPods are right for you?" (acknowledges the paradox, then resolves it)

## 3. Apple's Implicit Psychology

Apple never names these — but every page uses them.

| Tactic | How Apple does it |
|---|---|
| **Aspiration over feature** | "Take your inner artist out and about" vs. "Apple Pencil has low latency" |
| **Earn trust through specificity** | Numbers everywhere — 95%, 23 million, 4x, $1100 |
| **Identity confirmation** | Lines that say "this is for someone like you" |
| **Anchored value** | Trade-in credits are framed as generously as possible |
| **Everything is low-risk** | Free engraving, free returns, free Personal Setup — friction removal |
| **Simplicity signals quality** | Short headlines, white space, one claim per line |

## 4. PLFS Shortlist for Copywriters

These are the models most reliably effective in copy (vs. UI or pricing). Use first.

| Model | Typical PLFS | Best copy surface |
|---|---|---|
| Loss Aversion | 13–15 | Switching, trade-in, trial-ending, urgency |
| Identity / JTBD | 12–14 | Hero copy, lifestyle sections |
| Framing Effect | 11–14 | Pricing, value props, trade-in |
| Specificity (Authority) | 10–13 | Tech specs, sustainability, performance |
| Social Proof | 9–12 | "World's best", partner logos, usage volume |
| Anchoring | 10–13 | Pricing, trade-in value, comparison headlines |
| Paradox of Choice | 11–14 | Product selector copy, CTAs |

## 5. Psychological Tension Pairs

The most powerful copy often holds two ideas in tension. Apple masters this.

| Tension | Example |
|---|---|
| Powerful AND simple | "Smart. Secure. On device." — all three are separate |
| Premium AND affordable | "The magic of Mac at a surprising price." |
| Advanced AND accessible | "Help me choose." on the most advanced computer lineup |
| Minimal AND emotional | "Profound sound." — one word for deep emotion |
| Serious innovation AND playful language | "Magichromatic." "Pixel sharpener." |
| Individual AND universal | "iPhone for all." — inclusive but not generic |
| Technical AND human | "95 percent recycled lithium" → "More recycled content? Naturally." |

Use tension pairs when you want copy to feel sophisticated. Both things must be true — it's the tension that makes the reader lean in.
